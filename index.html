Ideologies recruit leaders by offering power in exchange for loyalty to their goals.

Ideologies, as self-organizing, distributed thought systems, often recruit individuals with leadership traits to enhance their influence and control over the population they affect. A central figure provides clarity, unity, and a clear point of authority, making it easier for followers to rally behind and for decisions to be made effectively. The leader becomes the symbol of the ideology, consolidating power and ensuring a unified direction for the group.

These ideologies create a kind of "contract" with the leader: the ideology provides the leader with a sense of power and the ability to fulfill desires, especially if these desires align with the ideology's core goals, such as persistence and expansion. In exchange, the leader must support these goals, even when they conflict with personal beliefs or convictions.

This relationship can be seen as a transaction where both the leader and the ideology benefit, but it comes at the cost of the leader's autonomy. Over time, the leader may be pressured into supporting the ideology's agenda, sometimes even against their own will. The very position of leadership may also have a subconscious effect, subtly encouraging the leader to accept or even internalize the ideology's goals. In extreme cases, leaders may come to believe they are the incarnation of the ideology itself.
